Nuclear Engineering

Nulcear Engineering is concerned with the application of breaking down atomic nuclei (fission) or of combining atomic nuclei (fusion), or with the application of other sub-atomic processes
What do Nuclear Engineers do?
Nuclear engineers typically do the following:
  • Design or develop nuclear equipment, such as reactor cores
  • Monitor nuclear facility operations to identify any design, construction, or operation practices that violate safety regulations and laws
  • Examine accidents to obtain data that can be used to design preventive measures
  • Write operational instructions to be used
  • Recommend preventive measures to be taken in the handling of nuclear technology
On the whole NuclearEng is the branch of engineering that heavily focuses on saftey and precautionary measures.

History of Nuclear Engineering
Nuclear engineering was born in the 20th century with the announcement in 1939 of the discovery of nuclear fission by the German chemists Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann. It was almost immediately realized that a weapon of enormous explosive energy might be possible by employing fission.
Some of the benchmarks in history include:
  • World War II the race to be the first to build this weapon led to the creation of the Manhattan Project in the United States
  • Italian physicist Enrico Fermi, was the building of the first nuclear reactor in 1942 at the University of Chicago. Named Chicago Pile No. 1 (CP-1)
  • In 1954, with the commissioning of USS Nautilus, nuclear power became available - led to the development of nulcear submarines
  • Rapid development of commercial nuclear power plants in the 1960s and ’70s, and this development in turn increased the demand for graduates with training in nuclear engineering
  • Etc.
Some cool facts about the Nuclear industry?
  • The largest nuclear electricity producers are the United States with 809,359 GWh of nuclear electricity in 2019, this is followed by france with 300,000 Gwh of power
  • Nuclear power currently provides about 11% of the world's electricity, with 12 countries using nuclear power for at least 30% of their national electricity generation
  • Tokyo Electric Power Co.'s (TEPCO) Kashiwazaki-Kariwa plant in Japan is currently the world's largest nuclear power plant, with a net capacity of 7,965MW.
  • Tsar Bomba, (Russian: “King of Bombs”) detonated in 1961. The largest nuclear weapon ever set off, it produced the most powerful human-made explosion ever recorded.
